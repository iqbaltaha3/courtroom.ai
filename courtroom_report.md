## 📚 Legal Research
- **Laws:** APPLICABLE SECTIONS:
1. Section 149 | Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)
   Relevance: This section deals with the liability of members of an unlawful assembly. It states that every member of an unlawful assembly is liable for any act done by another member in furtherance of the common object of the assembly if the act is committed in the prosecution of that object. The facts suggest a group (Malkhan, Kallu, and Mata Din) assembled with a shared intent related to the property dispute, and their actions resulted in Durga’s death.

2. Section 143 | Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)
   Relevance: This section defines an unlawful assembly as one formed for committing any mischief or illegal act, or intended to resist the lawful authority of a public servant. Whether the assembly of Malkhan, Kallu and Mata Din constitutes an “unlawful” assembly under BNSS will depend on whether their intent was to commit mischief or resist legal authority – arguably present given the longstanding property dispute and aggressive actions taken.

3. Section 302 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS)
   Relevance: This section defines murder. It states that causing death of another with intention to cause such death or with knowledge that it is probable that such death will occur constitutes murder. The prosecution alleges Malkhan struck Durga and all three accused repeatedly attacked him until he died, suggesting a possible intention or knowledge resulting in his death.

4. Section 304 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS)
    Relevance: This section addresses culpable homicide not amounting to murder. It covers acts done with intent to cause grievous hurt, and where death is caused due to such act, it can be classified as culpable homicide. The prosecution should consider this section if intention to kill cannot be proven beyond a reasonable doubt.

5. Section 324 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS)
   Relevance: This section deals with voluntarily causing hurt by dangerous weapons or means. The use of axes and sickles by the accused indicates a potential violation of this section, regardless of whether it leads to a murder conviction.

## 🛡️ Defense Round 1
## Defence Response – Round 1

My Lords/Ladyship, we have heard the Prosecution’s opening arguments outlining their case against Malkhan, Kallu, and Mata Din. While acknowledging the tragic loss of life in this unfortunate incident, we respectfully submit that the prosecution’s narrative is flawed, incomplete, and fails to adequately account for crucial mitigating circumstances and reasonable doubt. We intend to systematically dismantle their assertions and demonstrate that a conviction based on the evidence presented would be unjust and contrary to established legal principles.

1. **Challenge to the "Premeditated Act of Violence" Claim - Lack of Planning & Intent**

The prosecution paints a picture of premeditation – an act planned and calculated for malicious intent. However, there is *no* evidence of prior planning. The arrival of Malkhan, Kallu and Mata Din at the field was triggered by an existing dispute – not a prearranged attack. While tensions existed regarding the land, the Prosecution has offered no proof that these individuals conspired to harm Durga or Mullu. It remains entirely possible that their presence stemmed from a heated argument escalating into violence – a tragic clash fuelled by longstanding disagreement and not a coordinated plot as presented by the State. The *Balakrishnan* case emphasizes active participation; mere arrival at a scene does not constitute participation, especially absent evidence of pre-planning or a defined common objective beyond voicing displeasure about land ownership.

2. **Section 149 – Questioning “Unlawful Assembly” and Defining Common Object**

The prosecution aggressively invokes Section 149 (BNSS) by characterizing the group as an "unlawful assembly." This interpretation is premature, and we challenge its basis.  While a dispute undeniably existed, whether that dispute transformed the gathering into an “unlawful” assembly – one formed for mischief or resisting lawful authority - remains unproven under Section 143 (BNSS). It’s conceivable their intent was merely to discuss the land issue, an action permissible under law. More importantly, even if we concede the existence of a degree of illegality in their gathering, proving that *each* individual actively participated in furthering a common object related to Durga's death is critical for liability under Section 149. The Prosecution has failed to establish how Mata Din and Kallu, beyond allegedly being present, contributed to the fatal attack—a crucial element for applying constructive liability as clarified by *Ram Babu v. State of Bihar* (1980).

3. **Section 302 – Challenging Intent & Proposing Culpable Homicide**

The Prosecution’s assertion of “calculated intent” to murder under Section 302 is the most significant and, we contend, the least substantiated claim. While Durga sustained grievous injuries, the prosecution's reliance on repeated attacks to infer murderous intent is simplistic and ignores the chaotic nature of such confrontations. Violence often escalates in emotionally charged situations; a continued assault doesn’t automatically translate into a pre-meditated desire to kill. The *State of Punjab v. Joginder Singh* (1994) highlights factors considered when differentiating culpable homicide from murder – these include provocation, the degree of participation, and the nature of the weapon used. We argue that the evidence will demonstrate a loss of control driven by anger and frustration, supporting a charge of culpable homicide not amounting to murder under Section 304 (BNS), rather than the far more serious accusation of premeditated murder.

4. **Section 114 & Conversations: Potential for Misinterpretation and Unreliability**

The prosecution intends to introduce conversations between the accused under Section 114 (BSA). We are concerned about the potential for misinterpretation and unreliability. Conversations, especially in moments of heightened emotion, are easily distorted and can be taken out of context. Even assuming admissibility – which we contest based on chain of custody concerns - any such statements are likely to be ambiguous and susceptible to multiple interpretations. Furthermore, discussions may have been reactive—driven by the unfolding events rather than a pre-existing plot, further diminishing their probative value in establishing intent. The Prosecution must demonstrate that these alleged conversations unequivocally establish a shared intention to cause harm or death; failing this, relying on them risks convicting our clients based on speculation and conjecture instead of established fact.



We remain confident that upon full examination of the evidence, the court will find reasonable doubt sufficient to preclude convictions under any charge advanced by the prosecution. The burden remains squarely on the State – a burden they have yet to meet.

It's not necessary to prove Mata Din or Kallu wielded the axe; their presence and participation in the unlawful gathering actively furthered the shared goal of intimidating Durga into relinquishing his claim, a goal that tragically resulted in his death as established by the eyewitness testimony. The *Ram Babu v. State of Bihar* (1980) decision does not negate liability but emphasizes the need to establish connection between the common object and the illegal act which it seeks to achieve.

The *State of Punjab v. Joginder Singh* (1994) case highlights the crucial role of weapon usage; the deliberate choice of lethal weapons – axes and sickles – strongly suggests a desire to inflict fatal injuries. This was not a spontaneous clash; it was a targeted, relentless assault intended to silence Durga permanently. The very nature of their continued attacks on a fallen victim leaves no room for reasonable doubt regarding intent.

The potential for revealing a shared plan or motive within those conversations is substantial. Our chain of custody has been meticulously maintained, and we are prepared to address any challenge regarding admissibility. Even if their words contained ambiguity, coupled with the direct observation of witnesses detailing their aggressive demeanour towards Durga prior to their attack – they paint a consistent picture: intent and premeditation to cause grievous harm or death.
